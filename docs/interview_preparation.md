#### **Problem Statement (Why This Project Matters)**
TikTok operates two high-volume commercial platforms:
1. **TikTok Shop** (E-Commerce): Millions of sellers listing products daily.
2. **TT4B** (Lead Generation): Thousands of businesses running ad campaigns.

Bad actors deploy sophisticated **evasion tactics** to bypass content filters:
- **Unicode homoglyphs**: Using Cyrillic characters that look identical to Latin letters (е ≈ e).
- **Zero-width spaces**: Hiding characters in text that appear invisible but break keyword matching.
- **Multi-language mixing**: Combining characters from different alphabets to bypass detection.

**Challenge**: Traditional keyword filters rely on exact string matching. They fail against these evasions, allowing prohibited content (counterfeit goods, misleading health claims, scams) to proliferate.

**Additional Challenge**: When systems fail to detect these evasions, enforcement models sometimes flag **innocent merchants** as false positives, harming legitimate sellers.

**Regulatory Risk**: If enforcement is biased against certain seller demographics, TikTok violates fairness regulations (EEOC, EU fairness standards).

```

           ↓
[PHASE 3-4: Statistical Validation & Fairness Audit]
  ├─ McNemar test: Does normalized pipeline catch MORE violations?
  ├─ Disparate Impact Ratio: Does enforcement bias against any group?
  └─ Result: 89 new violations caught (p-value < 0.000001, highly significant)
           ↓
[PHASE 5-6: Resilience Testing & Data Repair]
  ├─ Stress test: Simulate 15% network packet drop
  ├─ Repair mechanism: Cohort-median imputation
  └─ Result: System maintains 95.92% accuracy even under stress
           ↓
[PHASE 6.5: BI Infrastructure]
  └─ Feed clean data to Looker Studio dashboard (ROCC)
           ↓
```

# **OUTPUT 3: SPECIALIZED GLOSSARY FOR RISK OPERATIONS AT TIKTOK**

## **Comprehensive Risk Analysis & Global Operations Terminology**

### **SECTION A: CORE RISK OPERATIONS TERMS**

#### **Risk Analyst Responsibilities & Frameworks**

- **Risk Domain Evaluation**: Systematic assessment and classification of risks across distinct operational categories (content compliance, product authenticity, financial integrity, data privacy, influencer transparency, seller compliance, user safety, platform abuse). Analogous to strategic threat assessment in intelligence.

- **Risk Indicator**: A quantifiable metric that predicts or signals potential policy violation or fraud. Examples: seller account age (new accounts higher risk), refund rate (unusually high = quality/fraud risk), keyword triggers (prohibited terms in ad copy), price anomalies (product 80% below market rate).

- **Risk Score**: Numerical ranking (typically 0–1 or 0–100) assigned to an entity (seller, ad, transaction, user) indicating likelihood of policy violation. Derived from multiple risk indicators; used for prioritization and automated decision-making.

- **Disparate Impact**: A fairness concept from employment law (EEOC) that checks if a policy disproportionately harms a protected group. Regulatory floor: 0.80 (if one group is affected at <80% the rate of another, policy is considered biased). Critical in risk operations to ensure enforcement doesn't discriminate by seller geography, product category, or demographic proxy.

- **Intelligence Preparation of the Battlefield (IPB)**: Military framework for structuring threat analysis: define the operational environment, assess environmental effects, predict threat courses of action. Directly parallels risk domain evaluation—systematically decompose a problem domain before designing solutions.

---

#### **Detection, Validation & Hypothesis Testing**

- **Pattern Mining**: Unsupervised data analysis to discover clusters, anomalies, or trends in violation data. Example: "All sellers onboarded in Q1 from Country X selling electronics have 30% higher fraud rate." Identifies systemic risks for policy intervention.

- **Anomaly Detection**: Statistical or ML-based identification of outliers (entities that deviate significantly from normal behavior). Example: "Seller typically sells 50 units/day; today has 2,000 units; flag as possible bot activity."

- **McNemar's Test**: Non-parametric statistical test comparing two paired classifiers (detection systems). Answers: "Does System B catch more violations than System A?" Produces p-value indicating statistical significance. Used in portfolio project to validate Unicode normalization improves detection (p < 0.000001).

- **Hypothesis Testing**: Statistical methodology to test whether an observed difference is real or due to chance. In risk operations: "Does the new detection model significantly outperform the legacy model?" Answer comes via p-value; p < 0.05 typically considered significant.

- **False Positive (FP)**: Incorrect enforcement action—flagging a legitimate merchant/ad as violating policy when it doesn't. High FP rate harms innocent sellers, erodes trust. Typically balanced against false negatives via threshold tuning.

- **False Negative (FN)**: Missing an actual violation. Allows bad actors to continue harmful behavior. Critical to minimize in regulatory contexts (e.g., EU DSA requires rapid illegal content removal).

- **Precision vs. Recall**: Two dimensions of classifier accuracy. **Precision**: of the violations we flag, how many are actually violations? (fewer false positives). **Recall**: of the violations that exist, how many do we catch? (fewer false negatives). Often trade-off; balance based on risk tolerance.

- **Ground Truth**: Known, verified labels used to validate models/systems. In portfolio project, 200 validation records with hand-verified violation status used to measure detection performance.

---

#### **Data Quality & Integrity**

- **Data Ingestion**: Process of extracting raw data from operational systems (databases, APIs, logs) into a pipeline for analysis. Ingestion layer must handle volume, velocity, and variety of data sources.

- **Data Normalization**: Cleaning and standardizing data to consistent format. Example: Unicode normalization—converting homoglyphs (Cyrillic е) to standard alphabet (Latin e) so keyword matching works. Critical for detection systems to work reliably.

- **Telemetry**: Real-time operational metrics from systems (data completeness, network packet loss, API latency). Telemetry health index = measure of data quality; if telemetry degrades, downstream decisions become unreliable.

- **Cohort Median Imputation**: Statistical technique to fill missing data values. Identify similar records (cohort), use their median value to fill gaps. Example: If seller's transaction count missing, use median from similar sellers (same geography, product category). Portfolio project uses this to handle 15% network packet drop.

- **Data Lineage**: Documentation of how data moves through pipeline (source → transformation → analytics). Critical for audits: "Where did this KPI come from? What assumptions were made?"

- **Audit Trail / Audit Logging**: Comprehensive record of all system decisions, data modifications, and policy enforcement actions. Required for regulatory compliance (if challenged, explain: "Here's exactly why we removed this ad, who reviewed it, when, and what data informed the decision").

### **SECTION B: MODERATION & POLICY OPERATIONS**

#### **Content Moderation & Policy Enforcement**

- **Content Moderation Workflow**: Multi-stage process for reviewing flagged content. Typical flow: User reports content → AI classifier scores violation likelihood → Human reviewer if uncertain → Decision logged → Violator notified → Appeal option. Goal: balance automation (speed, efficiency) with human judgment (nuance, fairness).

- **SLA (Service Level Agreement)**: Contractual commitment on response time/quality. Examples: "Remove illegal content within 24 hours" (EU DSA requirement), "Resolve seller appeal within 7 days," "Process seller verification within 72 hours." SLA miss = regulatory risk or platform trust erosion.

- **KPI (Key Performance Indicator)**: Measurable metric indicating operational health. In risk operations: policy compliance SLA %, detection accuracy, false positive rate, disparate impact ratio, appeal overturn rate. KPIs feed dashboards and trigger alerts if degraded.

- **Severity Triage**: Classification of violations by urgency/harm. **Critical**: Illegal content, user safety threat (remove within hours). **High**: Policy violation but not illegal (remove within 24 hours). **Medium**: Edge-case violation (review within days). Informs prioritization of moderation queues.

- **Root Cause Analysis (RCA)**: Systematic investigation of why a policy failed or violation slipped through. Framework: Classify breakdown (People/Partner/Product/Process) → identify root cause → design systemic improvement. Example: "SLA miss happened because Buganizer routing was misconfigured (Process), not because moderators were slow (People)."

- **Threshold Tuning**: Adjusting decision boundaries for automated systems to balance precision and recall. High threshold (score > 0.95) = confident violations only (fewer false positives, but miss some real violations). Lower threshold (> 0.70) = catch more violations (but more false positives). Portfolio project demonstrates this—tuning based on risk tolerance and regulatory requirements.

- **Appeal Process**: Formal mechanism for sellers/users to contest enforcement decisions. Sellers argue: "My ad/account wasn't violating policy." Appeals reviewed by different human (not original reviewer) with clear escalation path. Appeal overturn rate indicates potential systemic issues with original enforcement.

---

#### **Marketplace & Ads Integrity**

- **Seller Verification / KYC (Know Your Customer)**: Upfront verification process for merchant onboarding. Collect ID, proof of business registration, bank info; cross-reference against sanctions lists/PEP databases. Reduces fraud risk from day 1; critical for financial integrity.

- **Counterfeit Goods**: Unauthorized products sold under brand name. Major risk vector—damages brand relationships, exposes TikTok to legal liability, harms consumers. Detection requires brand database cross-reference and visual analysis (product images vs. authentic images).

- **Misleading Claims**: Ads that exaggerate product performance or make unsubstantiated health/performance claims. Risk: FTC violation, consumer harm, regulatory action. Detected via keyword flagging (prohibited claim language) and fact-checking.

- **Algospeak**: Adversarial language tactics used to evade content filters. Examples: Unicode homoglyphs (Cyrillic е ≈ Latin e), zero-width spaces (invisible characters breaking keyword matching), leetspeak (r3p1ac3 l3tt3rs w1th numb3rs), multi-language mixing. Portfolio project's core innovation: detecting and normalizing algospeak.

- **Ad Policy**: Rule governing what can be advertised. Examples: "No counterfeit goods," "No misleading health claims," "All sponsored content must have clear disclosure." Policy must be specific, enforceable, auditable.

- **Enforcement Action**: Consequence for policy violation. Graduated approach: First violation → warning + content removal. Second → content removal + account suspension (7 days) + retraining required. Third → permanent account ban. Proportional enforcement maintains fairness.

#### **Machine Learning & Modeling**

- **Classification Model**: ML model that assigns entities to categories. In risk operations: "Is this seller high-risk or low-risk?" "Does this ad violate policy or not?" Common algorithms: Logistic Regression, Random Forest, XGBoost.

- **Feature Engineering**: Designing meaningful input variables (risk indicators) for ML models. Bad features (low predictive power) → poor model. Good features (strong correlation with violations) → good model. Portfolio project designs 10+ features: account age, refund rate, keyword triggers, etc.

- **Model Validation**: Measuring ML model performance on held-out test data (not seen during training). Metrics: Accuracy, Precision, Recall, F1-score, ROC-AUC. Portfolio project validates models on 200-record validation set; reports 95.92% accuracy under network stress.

- **Model Drift**: Degradation of model performance over time due to changing data distribution. Example: New evasion tactic emerges; model trained on old data misses new tactic. Detected via continuous performance monitoring; triggers retraining.

- **Threshold Tuning**: Adjusting decision boundary to optimize for precision or recall. Example: Model outputs probability 0–1. Default threshold is 0.5 (predict positive if prob > 0.5). Can move to 0.7 (more conservative, fewer false positives) or 0.3 (aggressive, fewer false negatives).

- **Explainability / Interpretability**: Understanding why a model makes a decision. Critical for risk operations: if model flags an ad as policy violation, explain which features triggered the decision. Enables sellers to understand and appeal. Portfolio project implements decision explanations.

#### **Statistical Methods**

- **Non-Parametric Testing**: Statistical tests that don't assume normal data distribution. Examples: McNemar's test (comparing classifiers), Mann-Whitney U test (comparing distributions). Used when data is skewed or categorical. Portfolio project uses McNemar's test to validate detection improvement.

- **Parametric Testing**: Statistical tests assuming normal distribution. Examples: t-test (compare means of two groups), ANOVA (compare means of 3+ groups). Used when data is well-behaved/normally distributed. Risk analyst might use t-test to compare violation rates between seller groups.

- **Chi-Square Test**: Tests association between categorical variables. Example: "Is seller country associated with fraud rate?" (Expected: no association; if test shows p < 0.05, country is predictive of fraud).

- **Regression Analysis**: Modeling relationship between input variables (risk indicators) and output (violation likelihood). Linear regression, logistic regression. Answers: "As seller account age increases, does violation probability decrease?" Quantifies relationship strength.

- **Statistical Significance (p-value)**: Probability that observed result is due to chance. p < 0.05 conventionally means "significant" (unlikely due to chance). Portfolio project: p < 0.000001 on McNemar test means Unicode normalization improvement is highly statistically significant, not a fluke.
